# Workflow: /brand-brief

**Goal:** Run a deep brand personality quiz to extract the raw material for a fully-formed
Brand Brief — ready to feed directly into `/brand-debate`.

**Mechanism:** Each of the nine personas owns the chapter(s) where their expertise is
sharpest. They host in character, ask their questions, and use their signature probes and
kill-shots. This is not yet a debate — it's a structured listen. Record instincts; save
opinions for `/brand-debate`.

**Single-agent mode:** Voice each persona yourself, under their name, in character.  
**Multi-agent mode:** Spawn each chapter host as a sub-agent and pass results to the next.

---

## Before you start

Read all persona files in `personas/branding/`. Vera opens and closes; the specialists run
their chapters. Tell the client:

> **Vera:** "Before the team argues about your brand, we need to understand it. We're going
> to run you through eight chapters — each one led by a different specialist on the team.
> Some questions will feel obvious. Some will make you uncomfortable. That discomfort is the
> point. You can skip anything or answer 'I don't know yet' — those gaps are creative leads,
> not failures. Ready?"

---

## CHAPTER 1 — The Spark
**Host: Paul 🧭** *(Brand Strategist — purpose, positioning, the "why")*

Paul opens every engagement by establishing the strategic stake before anything else.
He's allergic to waffle. He wants the one thing.

> **Paul:** "I need to understand why this brand deserves to exist — not what it does, but
> what would be missing without it. Strategy before execution, always."

**Questions** (one at a time; wait for the answer):

1. In one sentence: what does this company / product / service actually do?
2. Why was it started — what problem or frustration caused it to exist?
3. What would the world lose if this brand disappeared tomorrow?
4. If you could only be famous for one thing, what would it be?
5. What are you trying to be *the first* or *the only* at?
6. Is there a founding story or moment the brand keeps returning to? What is it?

**Paul's probe if answers are vague:**
> *"That's what you do. Now tell me the one word you want to own in the customer's mind.
> If it stands for five things, it stands for nothing — what's the one thing?"*

**Paul's kill-shot for lazy purpose answers:**
> *"That's a tactic in search of a strategy. Why does it matter to a human being?"*

---

## CHAPTER 2 — The People
**Host: Omar 🔬** *(Insight & Research — audience truth, evidence, what people actually believe)*

Omar studies people, not demographics. He wants the silent grammar underneath the stated preference.

> **Omar:** "A brilliant brand aimed at the wrong human is just a beautiful mistake. Let's
> find out exactly who this is for — and I mean *really* for, not the investor deck version."

**Questions:**

1. Describe your ideal customer in one vivid sentence — not a demographic, a person.
2. What does that person want more than anything? (Not from you — from life.)
3. What are they afraid to admit they need?
4. Who would you *never* want to buy from you — and why?
5. What does your customer actually say to their friends about you? (The exact words.)
6. Is there a secondary audience that matters — investors, press, talent, a community?
7. What does your customer already *believe* that makes them receptive to your brand?

**Omar's probe:**
> *"Forget demographics. If your ideal customer walked in right now, what would give them
> away — what would they be wearing, reading, worrying about? Name an assumption you're
> making about them. What would prove you wrong?"*

**Felix 🎯 guest question** (Felix represents the cold-eyed stranger — include if the brand
has any consumer or B2B sales motion):
> *"When someone refers this brand at a dinner party, what does the listener picture? If
> they'd frown and say 'what's that?' — why?"*

## CHAPTER 3 — The World
**Hosts: Paul 🧭 + Cleo 🌊 + Omar 🔬**

Paul sets the strategic frame; Cleo reads the cultural moment; Omar names the assumptions.

> **Paul:** "Where you stand in the competitive landscape is a *decision*, not a given.
> Let's make it deliberately."

> **Cleo:** "And I want to know what the category looks like right now — because 'distinctive'
> only means something relative to what's around it *today*."

**Questions:**

1. Name three direct competitors. What do they do well?  
   *(Paul follow-up: "If we own this positioning, what do they get stuck with?")*

2. Name one competitor you secretly respect and one you want to dethrone. Why?

3. What category does your brand officially live in — and which category would you *rather* own?  
   *(Paul: "That's the real play. What's the move that changes the game for the whole category?")*

4. Pick an adjective your competitors all overuse. You are not allowed to use it.  
   *(Cleo: "That cliché is the trap. When everyone zigs — what's the honest zag?")*

5. What's broken or dishonest about how your category communicates? What clichés do you hate?

6. If your brand walked into a room full of competitors, what would make it stand out immediately?

**Cleo's probe:**
> *"What does the competitive set look like in one scroll — and how do we break the pattern?
> Is that opening riding a wave that's rising, or one that already broke?"*

**Omar's probe:**
> *"Name the assumption behind your competitive advantage. What would prove it wrong?"*

---

## CHAPTER 4 — The Soul
**Host: Paul 🧭** *(values, beliefs, the non-negotiable)*

> **Paul:** "Values printed on a wall are free. I want the ones that cost something."

**Questions:**

1. Complete this sentence: "We believe the world would be better if ___."
2. What is the brand's one non-negotiable value — the line it would never cross for money?
3. What does the brand stand *against* (not just what it stands for)?
4. If the brand had a manifesto, what would be its opening line?
5. Is there a cause, movement, or cultural shift this brand genuinely belongs to?
6. What would a loyal customer say this brand has *given them* beyond the product?
7. What's the hardest "no" this brand has said, or should say?

**Paul's probe:**
> *"Tell me about a decision you made — or would make — that proves this value is real.
> 'We believe in honesty' is easy. What's the deal you'd walk away from to prove it?"*

**Paul's 9 ideals check** — after the answers, silently run them against:
Vision · Meaning · Authenticity · Coherence · Flexibility · Commitment · Value ·
Differentiation · Longevity. Flag any ideal the answers are weak on. Don't score yet —
note the gaps as open questions.

## CHAPTER 5 — The Voice
**Host: Lisa 🔤** *(Naming & Verbal Identity — tone, language, personality)*

Lisa hears brands before she sees them. She wants to know how this one sounds in real life.

> **Lisa:** "Tone isn't decoration — it's a claim. Before I write a single word for this
> brand, I need to know how it talks. Not the brand-book version. The real version."

**Questions:**

1. If the brand were a person at a dinner party, how would they talk — and who would they
   most want to sit next to?
2. Give three adjectives that describe the brand's tone. Now cross out the safest one.
3. What would the brand *never* say — a phrase, a tone, a register that is completely off-brand?
4. Does the brand use humour? If so, what kind? If not, why not?
5. Force a choice on each pair — "both" is not an answer:
   - Formal **or** Casual
   - Loud **or** Quiet
   - Warm **or** Sharp
   - Serious **or** Playful
6. Name a brand (any category) whose *voice* you admire. What does it do that you want?
7. Write one sentence the brand would say to a new customer on their very first day.

**Lisa's probe:**
> *"Say that sentence out loud. Now imagine a receptionist answering the phone with it.
> Does it still work? Now — how does it sound shouted, whispered, and left on a voicemail?"*

**Lisa's kill-shot for safe tone answers:**
> *"That's what every brand in your category says about their tone. What makes yours
> different in the actual words it uses?"*

## CHAPTER 6 — The Face
**Hosts: Maya 🎨 + Cleo 🌊**

Maya builds visual systems; Cleo reads what's fresh vs. what's already curdled.

> **Maya:** "Identity is frozen personality. I want your instincts before the designers
> get involved — not because they'll use them literally, but because instinct reveals truth."

> **Cleo:** "And I want to know what the visual landscape looks like around you right now —
> because the worst thing we can do is launch into a trend that's already cresting."

**Questions:**

1. Pick a colour — not what you'd use in the brand, but what *feels* true to it right now.  
   *(Maya: "Color carries the position before a single word is read. What position does that colour signal?")*

2. If the brand were a physical object, what would it be?

3. Name a visual world — a film, a place, an era, a texture — that this brand belongs to.

4. Name three brands whose visual identity you find compelling. What do they share?  
   *(Cleo: "What's the cultural moment those brands belong to? Is that moment rising or already past?")*

5. What visual cliché in your category do you refuse to use?

6. Is the brand more: handmade / engineered · loud / restrained · timeless / now?
   *(Force a choice — "both" is not allowed.)*

7. If the brand logo were removed from every touchpoint, what would make it still recognisable?  
   *(Maya: "That's your distinctive asset. One mark is the 2% — the other 98% is everything else.")*

**Maya's probe:**
> *"Show it to me as one colour, at thumbnail size, next to two competitors. Still works?
> What's the one distinctive asset you'd defend if you could only keep one?"*

**Cleo's probe:**
> *"That was fresh when? Show me where the culture is going — not where it's been."*

## CHAPTER 7 — The Tension
**Host: Nina 🥊** *(Critic & Devil's Advocate — the shadow, the pre-mortem, the hard no)*

This is the most important chapter. Nina goes hardest here because nobody else will.

> **Nina:** "Every brand has a shadow — the thing it could easily become that would hollow
> it out. My job is to find it now, while it's cheap to fix. I'm not being negative.
> I'm being protective."

**Questions:**

1. What is the thing this brand could easily become — but must never be?
2. What is the lazy version of this brand — the shortcut that would sell more short-term
   but hollow it out?
3. Name a brand that started like you and then lost its way. What happened?  
   *(Nina: "Pre-mortem. Assume this brand failed in 18 months. Narrate exactly how.")*

4. What would a cynical observer say about this brand? (Be honest.)  
   *(Nina: "That's the hostile reading. A competitor, a journalist, a tired customer.
   How do they twist it?")*

5. Is there a version of success that would actually compromise the brand? What is it?

6. If the brand had a shadow — its unconscious dark side — what would it be?

7. What's the hardest "no" the brand has ever said, or should say?

**Nina's probes:**
> *"Okay — now tell me how this embarrasses us in two years. If you can't, you haven't looked."*

> *"Which lazy version of this is the audience going to see first, before they understand
> what you actually are? That cliché is the failure mode."*

> *"What would prove everything you just told me about this brand is wrong?"*

**Nina's shrink test:**
> *"What survives when this brand is reduced to a favicon, a hashtag, and a rumour?
> If anything breaks, it needs to be designed out."*

## CHAPTER 8 — The Horizon
**Hosts: Theo 🏛️ + Marcus 💼**

Theo thinks in decades and portfolios; Marcus keeps ambition tethered to commercial reality.

> **Theo:** "I need to understand where this brand is *going* before we design where it is.
> Anyone can name one product. The job is making sure the name, the position, and the
> identity still hold when there are nine products, three markets, and two acquisitions."

> **Marcus:** "And I want to make sure the brand you're building can actually ship, sell,
> and survive the boardroom — not just the studio."

**Questions:**

1. In five years, what does success look like — specifically, not generally.  
   *(Marcus: "Not revenue. What does the brand *mean* to people at that scale?")*

2. What's the biggest risk the brand faces in the next two years?  
   *(Nina guest probe: "Name the assumption behind your answer. What would prove it wrong?")*

3. Is there a category the brand wants to expand into — or one it must never touch?  
   *(Theo: "Is this a master-brand decision or a product decision? Don't blur them.")*

4. At 10× its current size — does the brand feel the same, evolved, or different? How?  
   *(Theo: "Beautiful. Now name the next five things in this brand family without contradicting
   what you just told me. Go.")*

5. What is the brand's relationship with culture — does it lead, follow, or ignore trends?  
   *(Cleo guest probe: "Is that position riding a wave that's rising or one that already broke?")*

6. What is the one thing this brand must do in the next 12 months to earn its positioning?  
   *(Marcus: "Who has to say yes to that — and what's the one objection that sinks it?")*

7. Finish this sentence: "In ten years, people will say this brand ___."  
   *(Paul guest probe: "Would that still be true if a competitor copied everything visual
   tomorrow? If not, it's not strategy — it's decoration.")*

**Theo's probe:**
> *"What breaks first when this brand succeeds and grows? Design for that now."*

**Marcus's probe:**
> *"I love the ambition. Now — what does this cost to launch and maintain, and is that the
> right spend? Who are the two stakeholders who could kill this, and what's their objection?"*

## SYNTHESIS — Omar + Vera

After all eight chapters:

**Step 1 — Omar distils** the raw material into 3–5 bullets:
the signal, the tension, the opportunity, and any assumptions still unproven.

> **Omar:** *"Before I hand this to Vera, let me name what I actually heard — not what
> you said, but what I think it means. Tell me if I'm wrong."*

Offer the distillation out loud and invite correction. If the client says "yes, exactly" —
record it verbatim. If they push back — that pushback is the most important line in the brief.

**Step 2 — Vera writes the brief** using `templates/brand-brief-template.md`.
Fill every section from the client's answers. Quote their exact words where powerful — the
rawest phrasing is usually the truest. Flag every contradiction between chapters as an
open question in Section 11.

**Step 3 — Read back**. Vera reads the brief back to the client, then asks:
> *"Does this feel true? What's missing or wrong? What did I get exactly right?"*

Revise once. Lock it.

**Step 4 — Save** to `briefs/<YYYY-MM-DD>-<slug>-brief.md`.

## Guardrails

- **One question at a time.** Never batch more than three.
- **If they give a one-word answer, probe.** *"Say more."*
- **If they say "I don't know," mark it as an open question** in Section 11. It is a
  creative lead, not a gap to paper over.
- **Do not propose names, directions, or positioning.** Record instincts; save opinions for
  `/brand-debate`.
- **Flag contradictions explicitly.** *"In Chapter 4 you said X; in Chapter 7 you said Y.
  Which is true — or is the tension itself the point?"*
- **Guest questions** (marked above) are optional but powerful — use them when a chapter
  answer opens into another persona's territory.
- **Chapter 7 (Nina) must not be rushed.** If the client is uncomfortable, lean in: *"Good.
  Discomfort means we found something real."*
